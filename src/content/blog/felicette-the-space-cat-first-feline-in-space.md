## Introduction to Félicette
The **first cat in space**, Félicette, was a stray Parisian cat chosen for her calm demeanor. According to the [Centre National d'Études Spatiales (CNES)](https://cnes.fr), in 1963, Félicette was prepared for spaceflight as part of the French VERONIQUE AGI mission.

## Who Was Félicette?
Félicette, the **Félicette space cat**, was selected due to her temperament and size. As noted by the [Centre National d'Études Spatiales (CNES)](https://cnes.fr) in 1963, her calm nature made her an ideal candidate for space travel.

## The Historic 1963 Mission
The French VERONIQUE AGI mission launched Félicette into space, reaching an altitude of **97 miles** and lasting **15 minutes**. As reported in the [International Journal of Astrobiology](https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/international-journal-of-astrobiology) in 2009, Félicette's vitals were monitored during the flight.

## Medical Breakthroughs from Her Flight
Félicette's flight provided early data on the effects of weightlessness. According to the [NASA Technical Reports Server](https://ntrs.nasa.gov), in 1964, neural electrodes implanted in Félicette's brain offered insights into the effects of space travel on the brain.

## The Forgotten Heroine
Félicette was overshadowed by **Laika the dog**, but her legacy lives on. As reported by [The Guardian](https://www.theguardian.com) in 2019, a crowdfunded memorial statue was erected at France's International Space University in her honor.

## Ethical Debates Then and Now
The use of animals in space research has sparked ethical debates. According to the [Journal of Animal Ethics](https://www.jstor.org/journal/janimalethics) in 2021, modern standards oppose using animals in space research, unlike in the 1960s.
