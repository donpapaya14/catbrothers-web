## Introduction to Cat Vision vs Human Senses Comparison
The **cat vision vs human senses comparison** is a fascinating topic that has garnered significant attention in recent years. According to a study by the [National Eye Institute](https://www.nei.nih.gov), 2019, cats have a unique visual system that allows them to see the world in a distinct way. Their retina contains a higher number of **rods** (120 million) compared to **cones** (7 million), which enables them to see better in low light conditions.

## The Amazing Acuity of Cat Vision
Cats have a **200-degree field of vision**, compared to humans who have a **180-degree field of vision**. This is due to the position of their eyes, which are located on the front of their head, allowing them to see more of their surroundings. The **National Eye Institute**, 2019, also found that cats have a higher concentration of **rods** in their retina, which are sensitive to movement and changes in light.

## Hearing: Cats vs Humans
Cats have an impressive **hearing range** of 50 Hz to 79,000 Hz, compared to humans who can hear sounds between 20 Hz and 20,000 Hz. According to the [American Society for the Prevention of Cruelty to Animals](https://www.aspca.org), 2020, cats can also detect sounds at a lower **decibel level** than humans. This is likely due to the unique structure of their **ear canal**, which is designed to amplify sound waves.

## Smell: Feline Olfactory Powers
Cats have a highly developed sense of smell, with **200 million olfactory receptors** in their nose, compared to only **6 million** in humans. The [Journal of Feline Medicine and Surgery](https://www.jfms.com), 2018, found that cats use their sense of smell to detect **pheromones** and other scents, which play a crucial role in their communication and behavior.

## The Trade-Offs of Cat Senses
While cats have exceptional senses, they also have some limitations. For example, their **color vision** is not as developed as humans, and they have difficulty seeing **high-pitched sounds**. According to [Scientific American](https://www.scientificamerican.com), 2015, this is due to the trade-offs that have evolved in their sensory systems, which prioritize certain types of sensory information over others.
