**Cat dental care preventing tartar** is crucial for maintaining your cat's overall health. ## Understanding Feline Dental Anatomy
The anatomy of a cat's teeth, gums, and jawbone is complex. As explained in the [AVMA 2020 Guidelines for Feline Dental Care](https://www.avma.org/KB/Policies/Pages/Guidelines-for-Feline-Dental-Care.aspx), cats have 30 permanent teeth, including incisors, canines, and molars. The gums and jawbone play a crucial role in supporting the teeth and maintaining oral health.

## AVMA Recommendations for Cat Dental Care
The AVMA recommends regular dental care for cats, including **brushing** and **dental exams**. According to the [AVMA 2020 Guidelines for Feline Dental Care](https://www.avma.org/KB/Policies/Pages/Guidelines-for-Feline-Dental-Care.aspx), cats should have a dental exam at least once a year. The [American Animal Hospital Association (AAHA)](https://www.aaha.org) also recommends brushing your cat's teeth daily to prevent **tartar buildup**.

## Recognizing Signs of Tartar Buildup and Gingivitis
**Tartar buildup** and **gingivitis** can have serious consequences for your cat's health. According to the [AVMA 2020 Guidelines for Feline Dental Care](https://www.avma.org/KB/Policies/Pages/Guidelines-for-Feline-Dental-Care.aspx), common signs of tartar buildup and gingivitis include **yellow or brown teeth**, **red gums**, and **bad breath**. If you notice any of these signs, it's essential to take your cat to a veterinarian for a dental exam.

## Professional Dental Cleanings and Anesthesia Safety
**Professional dental cleanings** are crucial for maintaining your cat's oral health. According to the [Journal of Veterinary Anesthesia and Pain Management (2019)](https://www.jvapm.com), anesthesia is necessary for professional dental cleanings. However, it's essential to choose a safe and qualified veterinarian to minimize the risks associated with anesthesia.
