## Introduction to Essential Vaccinations for Indoor Cats
The American Association of Feline Practitioners (AAFP) and the American Veterinary Medical Association (AVMA) recommend **core vaccinations** for indoor cats, including the rabies vaccination, to protect against serious diseases. According to the [American Association of Feline Practitioners](https://www.aafp.org), indoor cats are still at risk of contracting diseases such as **feline viral rhinotracheitis**, **calicivirus**, and **panleukopenia**.

## Which Vaccinations Are Required for Indoor Cats?
The AAFP and AVMA recommend the following **core vaccinations** for indoor cats:
* FVRCP Vaccine (for indoor cats): protects against feline viral rhinotracheitis, calicivirus, and panleukopenia
* Rabies vaccination: protects against rabies
As stated in the [American Association of Feline Practitioners Guidelines for Feline Vaccination (2020)](https://www.aafp.org), these vaccinations are essential for maintaining the health and wellbeing of indoor cats.

## When to Administer Vaccinations in Kittens
The American Animal Hospital Association (AAHA) recommends the following **vaccination schedule** for kittens:
1. First vaccination: 6-8 weeks old (FVRCP and rabies)
2. Second vaccination: 12-14 weeks old (FVRCP and rabies)
3. Third vaccination: 16-18 weeks old (FVRCP and rabies)
As noted in the [American Animal Hospital Association Vaccination Guidelines for Dogs and Cats (2020)](https://www.aaha.org), it is essential to follow this schedule to ensure optimal protection against diseases.

## Why Vaccination Boosters Are Crucial for Indoor Cats
Vaccination boosters are essential for indoor cats to maintain immunity against diseases. According to a study published in the [Journal of Feline Medicine and Surgery (2018)](https://www.sciencedirect.com/journal/journal-of-feline-medicine-and-surgery), **vaccination boosters** can help prevent the development of serious diseases such as feline leukemia and feline immunodeficiency virus.

## The Risks of Over-Vaccination in Indoor Cats
Over-vaccination can lead to adverse reactions in indoor cats, including **allergic reactions** and **autoimmune disorders**. As stated in the [American Animal Hospital Association Vaccination Guidelines for Dogs and Cats (2020)](https://www.aaha.org), it is essential to follow the recommended vaccination schedule to minimize the risks of over-vaccination.

## Non-Core Vaccinations for Indoor Cats: Are They Necessary?
Non-core vaccinations, such as the Feline Leukemia Virus (FeLV) and Feline Immunodeficiency Virus (FIV) vaccinations, may be recommended for indoor cats that are at high risk of exposure to these diseases. According to the [American Association of Feline Practitioners Guidelines for Feline Vaccination (2020)](https://www.aafp.org), these vaccinations can help protect against serious diseases.

## Consulting Your Veterinarian: A Key to Safe Vaccination
It is essential to consult with a veterinarian to determine the best vaccination schedule for your indoor cat, based on factors such as age, health status, and lifestyle. As noted in the [American Animal Hospital Association Vaccination Guidelines for Dogs and Cats (2020)](https://www.aaha.org), a veterinarian can help you make informed decisions about your cat's vaccination needs.
