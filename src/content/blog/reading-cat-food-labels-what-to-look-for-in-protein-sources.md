## Introduction to Cat Food Protein Sources
When it comes to **cat food protein sources**, it's essential to understand what to look for to ensure your feline friend is getting the best nutrition. According to the [National Research Council (NRC)](https://www.nap.edu), cats require a diet rich in protein from animal sources. 

## Understanding Meat Protein vs Plant-Based Protein
Meat protein and plant-based protein have different **digestibility** and **amino acid profiles**. Meat protein is generally more digestible and contains all essential amino acids, whereas plant-based protein may lack certain amino acids. A study by the [National Research Council (NRC)](https://www.nap.edu) in 2006 found that cats require a minimum of 30% of their daily calories to come from protein.

## Identifying Named Animal Sources vs Generic Terms
When reading cat food labels, look for **named animal sources** such as chicken, salmon, or beef, rather than generic terms like meat or poultry. According to the [Association of American Feed Control Officials (AAFCO)](https://www.aafco.org), named animal sources are more transparent and indicate higher-quality protein. Here are some examples:
* Chicken
* Salmon
* Beef
* Lamb

## Measuring Protein Quality: Protein Efficiency Ratio (PER)
The **Protein Efficiency Ratio (PER)** is a measure of protein quality, calculated by dividing the weight gain of a test subject by the amount of protein consumed. A higher PER indicates higher-quality protein. A study by the [University of Illinois](https://www.illinois.edu) in 2015 found that animal-based proteins have a higher PER than plant-based proteins.

## Plant-Based Protein Sources: Legumes, Grains, and Nuts
While plant-based protein sources like **legumes**, **grains**, and **nuts** can be nutritious, they may not provide all the essential amino acids cats require. According to the [Academy of Nutrition and Dietetics](https://www.andjrnl.org), legumes like peas and lentils are high in protein but may lack certain amino acids. Here are some examples of plant-based protein sources:
| Protein Source | Protein Content |
| --- | --- |
| Peas | 20-25% |
| Lentils | 18-20% |
| Rice | 7-8% |
| Corn | 8-10% |

## Label Claims: What 'High-Protein' Really Means
When a cat food label claims to be **high-protein**, it's essential to check the protein-to-fat ratio and overall nutrient balance. According to the [International Society of Animal Science](https://www.isas.us), a high-protein diet should have a protein-to-fat ratio of at least 2:1.
