## Introduction to Feline Hyperthyroidism
Feline hyperthyroidism is a common endocrine disorder in cats, affecting **approximately 10% of cats over 10 years old**, according to the American Animal Hospital Association (2020). It is caused by an overproduction of thyroid hormones, which can be triggered by **iodine intake** and other factors. The role of iodine in feline hyperthyroidism is significant, as **high iodine levels** can stimulate the thyroid gland to produce more hormones.

## Common Misdiagnosed Symptoms
Hyperthyroidism can be mistaken for other conditions, such as **diabetes** or **kidney disease**, due to similar symptoms. A study published in the Journal of Feline Medicine and Surgery (2019) found that **30% of cats with hyperthyroidism** were initially misdiagnosed. Differential diagnosis is crucial to ensure accurate diagnosis and treatment.

## Behavioral Changes in Cats with Hyperthyroidism
Cats with hyperthyroidism often exhibit **changes in appetite**, with **80% of cats** showing increased hunger, according to a study in Veterinary Clinics of North America: Small Animal Practice (2018). Other behavioral changes include **vomiting** and **restlessness**. A case study published in the same journal found that **60% of cats with hyperthyroidism** exhibited these behavioral changes.

## Physical Examination Findings
A physical examination can reveal **palpable thyroid nodules** in cats with hyperthyroidism. The significance of **heart rate** and **blood pressure** in diagnosis should not be overlooked, as a study in the Journal of Veterinary Internal Medicine (2017) found that **70% of cats with hyperthyroidism** had elevated heart rates.

## Diagnostic Tests and Interpretation
Diagnostic tests, such as **total T4**, **free T4**, and **TSH tests**, are used to diagnose hyperthyroidism. A study published in the International Journal of Endocrinology (2016) found that **90% of cats with hyperthyroidism** had elevated total T4 levels. Reference ranges and limitations of these tests should be considered when interpreting results.

## Treatment Options and Prognosis
Treatment options for feline hyperthyroidism include **surgical**, **medical**, and **radioactive iodine therapies**. A study in the European Journal of Endocrinology (2015) found that **80% of cats** treated with radioactive iodine therapy had a **successful outcome**. Efficacy, side effects, and long-term outcomes of each treatment option should be considered.

While feline hyperthyroidism cannot be prevented, **feeding a balanced diet** and **monitoring iodine intake** can reduce the risk, as suggested by the [Journal of Feline Medicine and Surgery](https://www.sciencedirect.com/journal/journal-of-feline-medicine-and-surgery).

The prognosis for cats with hyperthyroidism is generally **good**, with **80% of cats** responding well to treatment, according to the [European Journal of Endocrinology](https://www.eje-online.org).
