## Introduction to Raw Diets
A **raw diet for cats** involves feeding cats uncooked meat, bones, and other animal products. According to the [American Animal Hospital Association (2019)](https://www.aaha.org/), there are two main types of raw diets: prey model and commercial raw. The **prey model** aims to mimic the cat's natural diet by feeding whole prey, such as mice or rabbits, while **commercial raw** diets are pre-formulated and may contain a mix of meat, bones, and supplements. Many cat owners choose a **raw diet for cats** due to perceived health benefits, such as improved digestion and reduced allergy symptoms.

## Nutritional Risks of Raw Diets
Raw diets can pose nutritional risks, including **protein**, **fat**, and **vitamin imbalances**. A study published in the [Journal of the American Veterinary Medical Association (2018)](https://avmajournals.avma.org/) found that 60% of raw diets analyzed were deficient in one or more essential nutrients. For example, a **raw diet** may be low in **taurine**, an amino acid essential for feline heart health. To minimize these risks, it's crucial to consult with a veterinarian or a qualified animal nutritionist to ensure the diet is balanced and complete.

## Bacterial and Parasitic Risks
Raw diets can also pose **bacterial and parasitic risks**, including **Salmonella**, **E. coli**, and **Toxoplasma gondii**. According to the [Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (2020)](https://www.cdc.gov/), these pathogens can be transmitted to humans, particularly those with weakened immune systems. To reduce these risks, it's essential to handle and prepare raw diets safely, using proper storage and hygiene practices.

## Vet Consensus on Raw Diets
The [American Veterinary Medical Association (2020)](https://www.avma.org/) recommends that cat owners consult with their veterinarian before switching to a raw diet. Many veterinarians advise against raw diets due to the potential health risks, including nutritional deficiencies and bacterial contamination. However, some veterinarians may recommend **vet-approved raw cat food** that has been formulated to meet a cat's nutritional needs.

## Safe Handling and Preparation
To handle and prepare raw diets safely, follow these steps:
1. **Store raw meat and bones** in a sealed container at the bottom of the refrigerator to prevent cross-contamination.
2. **Wash hands** thoroughly with soap and water after handling raw meat and bones.
3. **Clean and disinfect** all surfaces and utensils that come into contact with raw meat and bones.
4. **Freeze raw meat and bones** for at least 3 days to kill parasites.
5. **Consult with a veterinarian** or a qualified animal nutritionist to ensure the diet is balanced and complete.
