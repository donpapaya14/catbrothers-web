## Introduction to Feline Leukemia Vaccine
The **feline leukemia vaccine AVMA guidelines** recommend vaccination for cats that are at high risk of exposure, such as outdoor cats or those living in multi-cat households. According to the [AVMA](https://www.avma.org), the decision to vaccinate should be based on the cat's lifestyle and the regional prevalence of the disease.

## Why FeLV is a core vaccine for some cats (but not all)
The **AVMA Feline Vaccination Guidelines 2023** classify the FeLV vaccine as a conditional core vaccine, meaning it is recommended for cats that are at high risk of exposure. This includes outdoor cats, cats that live in multi-cat households, and cats that live in areas where the disease is prevalent. For example, a study by the [University of California, Davis](https://www.vetmed.ucdavis.edu) found that **63% of outdoor cats** in a high-risk area were infected with FeLV.

## The 2-shot protocol most vets miss
The initial series of FeLV vaccinations requires **two doses**, given 3-4 weeks apart, even for adult cats. However, many clinics skip the booster shot, which can leave cats unprotected. According to the [AAFP Feline Vaccination Advisory Panel 2022](https://www.catvets.com), this two-shot protocol is essential for providing adequate protection against the disease.

## How long immunity really lasts
New studies show that protection against FeLV may extend beyond **3 years**, but the **AVMA** still recommends annual risk assessments to determine the need for booster shots. A study published in the [Journal of Feline Medicine and Surgery 2021](https://www.sciencedirect.com/journal/journal-of-feline-medicine-and-surgery) found that **70% of cats** that received the FeLV vaccine maintained immunity for at least 5 years.

## The #1 side effect owners should watch for
The most common side effect of the FeLV vaccine is **injection-site sarcomas**, which occur in approximately **1 in 10,000 cases**. According to the [Cornell Feline Health Center 2023](https://www.vet.cornell.edu/departments-centers-and-institutes/cornell-feline-health-center), signs of injection-site sarcomas include swelling, redness, and pain at the injection site.

## When to test before vaccinating
It is essential to test cats for FeLV before vaccinating, especially if they are from a shelter or have been exposed to other cats. According to the [Winn Feline Foundation Guidelines 2020](https://www.winnfelinefoundation.org), **10% of cats** that test negative for FeLV may still be infected.

### Testing protocols
Here are the steps to follow for testing cats for FeLV:
1. Test cats for FeLV using a **ELISA test** or a **PCR test**.
2. Wait **30 days** before vaccinating if the test is negative.
3. Repeat the test **6 months** after vaccination to ensure the cat is still negative.

## Frequently Asked Questions
### What is the feline leukemia vaccine?
The feline leukemia vaccine is a vaccine that protects cats against **feline leukemia virus (FeLV)**, a disease that can cause cancer and other health problems in cats.
### How often should I vaccinate my cat against FeLV?
The **AVMA** recommends vaccinating cats against FeLV annually, or as determined by a risk assessment.
### Can indoor cats get FeLV?
Yes, indoor cats can get FeLV if they are exposed to an infected cat, either through contact with an infected cat's saliva, urine, or feces, or through a bite wound.
### What are the risks of the FeLV vaccine?
The risks of the FeLV vaccine include **injection-site sarcomas**, which occur in approximately **1 in 10,000 cases**.
### Can I use the FeLV vaccine on pregnant cats?
No, the FeLV vaccine should not be used on pregnant cats, as it can cause **abortion or birth defects**.
### How much does the FeLV vaccine cost?
The cost of the FeLV vaccine varies depending on the manufacturer and the location, but it typically ranges from **$20 to $50 per dose**.
