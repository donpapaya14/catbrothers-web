## Introduction to Feline Leukemia Vaccine Schedule for Indoor Cats
Indoor cats are at a lower risk of contracting **feline leukemia virus (FeLV)**, but they still need protection. According to the [Cornell Feline Health Center](https://www.vet.cornell.edu/departments-centers-and-institutes/cornell-feline-health-center), 30% of FeLV cases occur in indoor cats. The **feline leukemia vaccine** is crucial for indoor cats due to potential transmission risks via windows/screens, accidental escapes, and vet visit exposures.

## Why Indoor Cats Still Need FeLV Protection
The risk of FeLV transmission to indoor cats is significant, with 30% of cases occurring in cats that do not venture outdoors, as reported by the [Cornell Feline Health Center](https://www.vet.cornell.edu/departments-centers-and-institutes/cornell-feline-health-center) in 2021. This highlights the importance of the **FeLV vaccine schedule** for indoor cats.

## AVMA's 2024 Two-Dose Kitten Protocol
The American Veterinary Medical Association (AVMA) recommends a two-dose kitten protocol for the **feline leukemia vaccine**. The initial dose should be administered between **8-12 weeks** of age, followed by a booster dose **3-4 weeks later**. Delaying the booster dose until the cat is 1 year old can leave a gap in immunity, making the kitten more susceptible to FeLV infection.

## The 3-Year Booster Debate Explained
For adult cats, the debate surrounding the need for **3-year boosters** versus **titre testing** continues. According to the [UC Davis School of Veterinary Medicine](https://www.vetmed.ucdavis.edu/), titre testing can be a more cost-effective option, with a study showing that only 20% of cats required a booster dose after 3 years. However, the decision to use titre testing or routine boosters should be made on a case-by-case basis, taking into account the individual cat's risk factors and health status.

## Signs Your Cat Needs an Earlier Booster
Certain behavioral changes and household factors can increase the risk of FeLV transmission to indoor cats. These include:
1. Sudden aggression
2. Hiding or avoidance behavior
3. Introduction of new cats to the household
4. Construction noise or other environmental stressors
If you notice any of these signs, consult with your veterinarian to determine if an earlier booster is necessary, as advised by the [Journal of Feline Medicine](https://www.sciencedirect.com/journal/journal-of-feline-medicine).

## Vaccine Alternatives for Immunocompromised Cats
For immunocompromised cats, such as those with **FIV (feline immunodeficiency virus)**, alternative vaccine options may be necessary. The [Tufts University Cummings School](https://cummings.vet.tufts.edu/) recommends the use of recombinant vaccines, which can provide immunity without the risks associated with traditional vaccines. Additionally, strict isolation protocols may be necessary to prevent FeLV transmission.

## Frequently Asked Questions
### What is the most common way for indoor cats to contract FeLV?
The most common way for indoor cats to contract FeLV is through exposure to an infected cat, either through a window or screen, or during a vet visit. According to the [World Health Organization](https://www.who.int), FeLV is a significant health risk for cats worldwide.

### How often should I vaccinate my indoor cat against FeLV?
The **AVMA** recommends a two-dose kitten protocol, with a booster dose administered 3-4 weeks after the initial dose. Adult cats may require a 3-year booster, but this should be determined on a case-by-case basis.

Yes, recombinant vaccines are available for immunocompromised cats, and they can provide immunity without the risks associated with traditional vaccines.
