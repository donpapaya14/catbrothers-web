## Introduction to Feline Vaccinations AVMA Protocol
The American Veterinary Medical Association (AVMA) provides guidelines for **feline vaccinations**, emphasizing the importance of core and non-core vaccines in maintaining **cat health**. According to the AVMA 2020 Guidelines for Vaccination of Dogs, Cats, and Rabbits, core vaccines include the rabies vaccine, while non-core vaccines are determined based on the individual cat's risk factors.

## Understanding the Core and Non-Core Vaccine Recommendations
The AVMA recommends that all cats receive the **rabies vaccine**, as it is a core vaccine. Non-core vaccines, such as those for feline leukemia virus (FeLV) and feline immunodeficiency virus (FIV), are recommended based on the cat's lifestyle and risk factors. For example, a cat that spends time outdoors may require the FeLV vaccine.

| Vaccine | Core/Non-Core | Description |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Rabies | Core | Protects against rabies virus |
| FeLV | Non-Core | Protects against feline leukemia virus |
| FIV | Non-Core | Protects against feline immunodeficiency virus |

## Vaccine Schedule for Kittens and Adult Cats
The recommended vaccine schedule for kittens and adult cats varies based on age and risk factors. Kittens typically receive their first vaccinations at 6-8 weeks of age, with boosters given every 3-4 weeks until they are 16-17 weeks old. Adult cats require annual vaccinations, with the frequency of non-core vaccines determined by their individual risk factors.

1. Kittens: first vaccinations at 6-8 weeks, boosters every 3-4 weeks until 16-17 weeks old
2. Adult cats: annual vaccinations, with non-core vaccines given as needed

## Rabies Vaccine Requirements and Exemptions
The **rabies vaccine** is required by law in most states, with exemptions made for cats that are medically unable to receive the vaccine. According to the AVMA, cats that are exempt from the rabies vaccine must have a written waiver from a licensed veterinarian.

## Feline Leukemia Virus (FeLV) and Feline Immunodeficiency Virus (FIV) Testing
Testing for **FeLV** and **FIV** is recommended for all cats, particularly those that are at high risk of infection. The American Association of Feline Practitioners (AAFP) recommends testing all cats for FeLV and FIV, with repeat testing performed annually for high-risk cats.

## Vaccine-Associated Adverse Events (VAE) and Reporting Requirements
**Vaccine-associated adverse events** (VAE) can occur in cats, although they are rare. According to the AVMA, VAE can include reactions such as vomiting, diarrhea, and lethargy. If a VAE occurs, it must be reported to the vaccine manufacturer and the AVMA.

## Consulting with Your Veterinarian and Staying Up-to-Date on Vaccine Recommendations
It is essential to consult with your veterinarian about **vaccine recommendations** for your cat, as they can provide personalized advice based on your cat's individual needs. Staying up-to-date on the latest vaccine guidelines is also crucial, as recommendations can change over time.

### Tips for Consulting with Your Veterinarian
1. Schedule annual check-ups with your veterinarian to discuss vaccine recommendations
2. Provide your veterinarian with your cat's complete medical history
3. Ask your veterinarian about the risks and benefits of each vaccine
