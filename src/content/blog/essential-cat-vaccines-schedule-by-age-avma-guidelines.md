## Introduction to Essential Cat Vaccines by Age
The American Veterinary Medical Association (AVMA) outlines a clear **essential cat vaccines by age** schedule to protect your feline friend from deadly diseases. According to the [AVMA](https://www.avma.org), core vaccines include **rabies** and **FVRCP (feline viral rhinotracheitis, calicivirus, panleukopenia)**, which are considered absolute musts for all cats.

## Core vs. Non-Core Vaccines: What AVMA Says
The AVMA distinguishes between core and non-core vaccines based on the risk of exposure and the severity of the disease. Core vaccines, such as **rabies** and **FVRCP**, are universally recommended, while non-core vaccines, like **FeLV**, are given based on the cat's lifestyle and risk factors. The AVMA provides a [risk-assessment flowchart](https://www.avma.org/resources-tools/avma-vaccination-principles-2023) to help veterinarians determine which non-core vaccines are necessary for each cat.

## Kitten Vaccination Timeline Week-by-Week
The [AAFP Feline Vaccination Advisory Panel 2022](https://www.catvets.com/guidelines/practice-guidelines/feline-vaccination) recommends the following **kitten shot timeline**:
| Age | Vaccine |
| --- | --- |
| 6-8 weeks | First FVRCP |
| 10-12 weeks | Second FVRCP |
| 12-16 weeks | Rabies |
| 14-16 weeks | Third FVRCP |
| 16 weeks | Booster shots |

## Adult Cat Booster Shots Most Owners Miss
Adult cats require **booster shots** to maintain immunity against diseases. The [Journal of Feline Medicine 2021](https://www.sciencedirect.com/journal/journal-of-feline-medicine) suggests that **rabies** and **FVRCP** boosters are needed every 3 years, while some vaccines may require annual boosters. A study by [Tufts University Vet Economics Study 2020](https://vet.tufts.edu/about/news-events/news-archive/2020/02/vaccine-titers/) found that **vaccine titers** can replace some boosters, reducing the cost and risk of over-vaccination.

## Indoor-Only Cats: Which Vaccines You Can Skip?
The AVMA recommends that **indoor-only cats** receive core vaccines, but may not need non-core vaccines like **FeLV**. However, if you plan to introduce a new cat to your household or if your indoor cat is at risk of escape, your veterinarian may recommend additional vaccines. The [AVMA Lifestyle-Based Vaccine Guide 2023](https://www.avma.org/resources-tools/avma-lifestyle-based-vaccine-guide-2023) provides guidance on vaccine selection based on lifestyle and risk factors.

## Cost-Saving Tips Without Compromising Safety
To save money on vaccinations without compromising your cat's safety, consider the following tips:
1. Use **vaccine titers** to determine if your cat needs a booster shot.
2. Take advantage of **low-cost clinic options** in your area.
3. Avoid **bundled packages** that may include unnecessary vaccines.
The [Tufts University Vet Economics Study 2020](https://vet.tufts.edu/about/news-events/news-archive/2020/02/vaccine-titers/) found that these strategies can help reduce the cost of vaccinations without compromising safety.

## Dangerous Vaccine Reactions to Watch For
While vaccines are generally safe, there is a risk of **vaccine-associated sarcoma (VAS)** and other adverse reactions. If you notice any of the following symptoms after vaccination, seek veterinary care immediately:
* **Lethargy**
* **Loss of appetite**
* **Vomiting**
* **Diarrhea**
The [Cornell Feline Health Center 2023](https://www.vet.cornell.edu/departments-centers-and-institutes/cornell-feline-health-center) provides guidance on recognizing and responding to vaccine reactions.
